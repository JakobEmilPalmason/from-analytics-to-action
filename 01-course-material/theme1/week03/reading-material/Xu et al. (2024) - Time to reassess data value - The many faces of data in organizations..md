A B S T R A C T
Despite the substantial body of evidence detailing the multifaceted use of data within organizations, the conceptualizations of data and their value 
propositions remain disjointed and require updating. Information Systems scholars contend that the traditional ways of conceiving data now appear 
inadequate in framing this ever-evolving data-driven phenomenon. In this context, we argue for a reassessment of the fundamental assumptions 
about data in the field. This paper offers a comprehensive literature review, through which we conceptualize the role of data into four distin­
guishable types: data as a tool, as a commodity, as a practice, and as algorithmic intelligence. Each type possesses a set of identifiable characteristics, 
usage, and unique pathways of value creation. Together these elements form a typology, which provides an explanation for the intricate and 
complex nature of data use in organizations and the diverse sources of their value.
Introduction
In today’s business landscape, everything is data — not only the static, structured data collected from customers, employees, 
operations, and transactions, but also the continuously updating, unstructured data from social media, enterprise systems, and other 
platforms (Baesens et al., 2014; Constantiou and Kallinikos, 2015). Additionally, data may also include the digital imprints that users 
leave when interacting with devices, facilities, people, and organizations in their everyday lives (Gregory et al., 2021). These diverse 
forms and sources of data can imply substantial value by addressing specific organizational objectives (Abbasi et al., 2016; Grover 
et al., 2018).
While significant progress has been made by Information Systems (IS) researchers in recognizing the strategic importance of data 
for organizations (Brynjolfsson and McElheran, 2016; Chen et al., 2012; Grover et al., 2018; Hagiu and Wright, 2020), robust evidence 
has shown that the ways in which data contribute to business have evolved over time and grown increasingly varied (Aaltonen et al., 
2023; Xu et al., 2023). Data can be used to inform and improve managerial decision-making (Brynjolfsson and McElheran, 2016; 
Ghasemaghaei, 2019). Data can also be embedded in organizational processes to shape digital innovations in products, process, and 
business model (Hagiu and Wright, 2020; Gregory et al., 2021; Grover et al., 2018). Additionally, data can be shared across organi­
zations, or traded to generate direct revenue (Aaltonen et al., 2021). Data can even simulate human intelligence to automate business 
processes, and prescribe programmed actions without human interventions (Shollo et al., 2022). We argue that the diverse usage of 
data in organizations reveals the changing nature and multiple pathways of value creation, which needs to be addressed and managed 
accordingly.

and their value. First, there has been limited explorations of data regarding their nature, effects, and character, upon which the data 
phenomena are based (Zins, 2007; Jones, 2019). Up until today, conceptualizations of data are still found to be “bedevilled by in­
consistencies” (Jones, 2019). At the same time, the engineering-oriented conception of data has dominated IS (Alaimo et al., 2020), 
and this may have fallen short of explaining their “intangible nature” (Faulkner and Runde, 2019) in the organizational context. For 
example, while research identifies an “objective and homogeneous” nature in the big data context of strategizing (Constantiou & 
Kallinikos, 2015), it also argues that the use context (or “situated practice”) is the key variable in this loop, as discussed by Jones 
(2019) — “unless data are sought, selected, extracted, and interpreted [for a purpose], they cannot inform”. Given the diversity and 
complexity of data use in nature, we recognize the field needs to make further efforts to consolidate and reflect on what has been 
discussed. Absence of this kind of research could result in “pretheoretical understandings” (Ekbia, 2009) of the entities involved, “with 
the potential lack of clarity, detail and nuance” (Faulkner and Runde, 2019).
Second, despite the varied ways that data are used to create value in organizations, the approach to conceive and measure data 
value has not kept pace but remains outdated, and disjointed (Aaltonen et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023). For years, the data-information- 
knowledge-action value chain has had a strong presence in IS research. However, the latest developments in data characteristics have 
transcended this boundary (Abbasi et al, 2016). Grounded from IT/IS value research, data has been consistently regarded as a strategic 
resource of organizations (Chen et al., 2012; Günther et al., 2017; Surbakti et al., 2020), emphasizing resource allocation and 
orchestration as the core to realize data value (Ghasemaghaei, et al., 2015; Ghasemaghaei and Calic, 2020; Trieu, 2017). More recent 
literature also identifies data as an intangible asset contributing to knowledge creation and improving decision-making (Shollo and 
Galliers, 2016; Cˆorte-Real et al., 2017). While these perspectives have been instrumental in recognising the impact of data use on 
business outcomes (Trieu, 2017), it may not suffice to capture the intricate nature of data and their dynamic capabilities in 
contemporary contexts. In the era of big data, increased volume leads to complexities, not necessarily better decisions (Abbasi et al., 
2016); data can now be widely reused and shared without diminishing its value, challenging the fundamental economic assumptions of 
data value (Jones and Tonetti, 2020). Therefore, scholars argue that any initiatives of value capture and measurement should consider 
the nature of data use specific to their purposes and contexts (Aral and Weill, 2007; Xu et al., 2023). In a recent discussion in the data 
research community about “what is missing from research on data in the field of IS” (Aaltonen et al., 2023), scholars advocate that the 
core of IS research needs to center around exploring the pivotal role of data: “this involves fostering critical and innovative scholarly 
inquiry into data, spanning the intersections between IS and adjacent fields”.
To address these issues in the current body of knowledge, this study conducts a comprehensive literature review aimed at 
examining the diverse and up-to-date use of data in organizations and integrating a unique set of theoretical assumptions about the 
characteristics of data. We pose the following research question: In what ways can data contribute to business objectives, and how can we 
conceptualize the role of data in organizations? Our research aims to offer a twofold contribution. First, we broadly examine and syn­
thesize the existing assumptions in the field concerning data. By bridging multiple perspectives and dimensions, we develop a typology 
that elucidates the versatile roles that data play in organizations, demonstrating the distinctive characteristics associated with each 
role. Second, we discuss how this elevated treatment of data within the scholarly community can assist practitioners in leveraging this 
deeper understanding as they seek to effectively monetize, manage, and govern data assets in their organizations.
This article is structured into six core sections. Following this introduction, we explain our perspective in reviewing data in or­
ganizations and the stances we adopt in this investigation. Next, we explain the review methodology, and how we proceed to syn­
thesize the versatile roles of data that emerge from the literature. Later, we present a typology of identified roles and characteristics of 
data, followed by a discussion of practical and theoretical implications of these findings.
A new perspective on data in organizations
Our intention to draw attention to the roles of data has been inspired by Orlikowski and Iacono (2001), who appeal for the need to 
develop more sophisticated understandings regarding the role of IT artifacts. The recent discussions of Faulkner and Runde (2019)
about digital objects have also sparked further reflection. Both emphasize the central role of technology within larger systems in which 
they operate and interact with. IT artifacts – typically packaged in tangible forms such as hardware and/or software – are always 
embedded in a context (Benbasat and Zmud, 2003), and are not “natural, neutral, universal, or given” (Orlikowski and Iacono, 2001). 
According to Faulkner & Runde (2019), digital objects can in some way resemble people in the sense that they hold social positions 
within larger systems. These positions, among other “nonmaterial qualities”, are interconnected, and can significantly shape the social 
identity of the individuals involved. Hence, it is important to take a holistic view in examining the interplay between key enablers: 
technology, people, and the organization. The dynamic interactions among them drive outcomes throughout the various stages of use 
within the organization (Orlikowski and Iacono, 2001; Hevner et al., 2014). We summarize our perspective by highlighting the two 
critical positions that we take in this investigation.
First, we explore a wide array of data usage in organizations, placing particular emphasis on the purpose and context of use. Robust 
evidence has shown that data have been used in many ways in organizational contexts and served multiple business objectives (Xu 
et al., 2023). According to Aral and Weill (2007), a strategic purpose is vital, which highlights what specific business objectives the IT 
assets are to support within an organization. DeLone and McLean (2003), in their ten-year review of the IS Success Model, contend that 
“merely stating that more use will yield more value, without considering the nature of this use, is clearly insufficient”; therefore, 
researchers must also consider “the nature, extent, quality, and appropriateness of the system use”. Building upon this perspective, we 
further unpack our research question into a series of inquiries: What specific business purposes are served by using data? What business 
value does data contribute to? In what way is the data value assessed or reflected? What other factors, such as people and organizational 
practices, may play a role in the value creation? As we contemplate these questions throughout the literature review, we aim to

To make sense of these diverse but nuanced phenomena, we have drawn on the theoretical lens of Orlikowski and Iacono (2001)’s

broad view, rather than confining the discussions narrowly.
Review method and process
In this section, we outline the process and methods used for searching, selecting, and analyzing literature for our review. Our 
approach to the literature review aligns with the broad-theorizing review guidelines introduced by Leidner (2018). Distinguished by 
research focus or objective from three other major types— organizing reviews, assessing reviews, and specific-theorizing reviews — 
the broad-theorizing approach organizes and describes the literature, aiming to use the synthesis and description as a “springboard” for 
theorizing (Leidner, 2018). By adopting this approach, we assert that our method is integrative in nature, as our efforts are focused on 
integrating existing knowledge previously dispersed in the literature (Leidner, 2018; Cronin and George, 2023). On the Breslin and 
Gatrell (2023) “miner-prospector continuum” used to classify the types of literature reviews, our approach can be best described as 
prospectors, rather than miners. Prospectors may “take a broader view”, “venture outside disciplinary boundaries”, hoping to intro­
duce new perspectives or conceptualizations. A prospector approach may involve strategies of transferring theories across domains, 
blending and merging ideas that may seem at first unconnected (Breslin and Gatrell, 2023). We have found these strategies very 
relatable in our review process.
Given the extensive scope of this study, we strive for comprehensiveness by using a structured process, or using an algorithm, to 
search and select the literature, considering both conceptual and empirical contributions (Par´e et al., 2015; Cronin and George, 2023). 
Yet, we are also mindful of balancing between a comprehensive set of relevant literature and the quality, importance, and the “level of 
faith” in reporting them (Cronin and George, 2023). Below, we describe our review process according to the three key steps in which it 
was conducted.
The first step involves scoping the topic and identifying its boundaries. We began with extensive reading and immersed ourselves in the 
literature to gain a broad understanding of the focal topic, key concepts, and research streams. To facilitate our scoping process, we 
created mind maps, which provide a sketch of discussions and key concepts covered in the literature, helping us gauge the breadth of 
the topic. It was evident that data have been discussed diversely in literature and concepts are interwoven. With a strategic orientation 
towards leveraging data to improve organizational performance, we focused our study on Information Systems, and neighboring areas 
such as Business Management, Strategy, Organization, and Economics. The inclusion of publications in these adjacent fields ensures 
that we did not miss essential concerns and arguments in these cognate disciplines. We also included proceedings of premier con­
ferences in the fields of IS and Management that have occurred within the last five years. This ensures that we do not overlook 
publications that have yet to be included in journals due to the lengthy review cycle.
We crafted search keywords to cover all related discussions about data use and their associated value or impact for organizations. 
We used algorithmic search strings that contain variations of keywords: “data business value or strategic value”, “data value or 
valuation”, “data (or analytics) and performance (or impact)”, “data monetization”, “data commodity”, and “data marketplace”. We 
compared different sources of literature and made decisions over the databases, and inclusion and exclusion criteria. Some critical 
considerations of these decisions are explained in the methodology details Appendix (Appendix B, Table B-1).
The second step is literature search and selection. We used Web of Science as the main database, supplemented by AISeL and some 
journal websites such as MIS Quarterly and The Journal of Strategic Information Systems. The initial search returned 3,508 academic 
publications with our filtering criteria excluding non-peer-reviewed lecture notes and book chapters. We then conducted a manual 
screening of each article to identify its relevance to our study, considering several factors. To begin with, we assessed the content by 
title and abstract, considering its relevance to answering our research questions. While there is a vast related body of literature, our 
study focuses on investigating the roles and value of data, thereby limiting our search primarily to the data business value domain and 
the motivational aspect of data use (Surbakti et al., 2020). Specifically, we looked for papers that mentioned a data use purpose or 
context and possible business outcomes. We then excluded investigations of non-organizational context or overly narrow business 
scenarios; this helped to restrict the discussions to an organizational setting and enabled mid-range generalizability of our research 
findings. Given that data have been discussed very broadly, this step excluded many papers from the initial dataset. Furthermore, we 
conducted a backward and forward snowballing process (Webster and Watson, 2002) to include more literature via a bibliometric co- 
citation approach. We used seminal papers collected in earlier stages as concept-centric “seeds” to identify those who cited them or 
those whom they cited, and to identify links in concept development. We also performed targeted searches for missing papers that 
included more recent adoptions of big data analytics (BDA), AI, and machine learning. The final selection of the sample included 142 
papers from 41 peer-reviewed journals and 8 premier conference proceedings. Details about the literature selection process and

The third step is literature sorting, coding, and analysis. Given our objective to describe a complex data phenomenon and to critically 
synthesize theoretical arguments, we have come to realize that it is necessary to adopt different analytical methods at different stages 
of our analysis, situating this vast body of literature. The initial stage is akin to an interpretive qualitative way of organizing literature 
(Leidner, 2018), aiming to sensitize the phenomena. Specifically, we began by familiarizing ourselves with the setting and making 
inquiries about themes in a fairly open way: what is going on there? First, we started with a sample of empirical papers and conducted a 
set of ordered steps of open coding and sorting, with an interest in observing the phenomena of data practices in organizations. We 
applied a set of criteria in literature coding – such as research methods, keywords, and concepts or theories. We also extracted in­
formation and observed aspects of business purposes of data use, people (i.e., users, decision makers, and their roles), and organi­
zational practices (i.e., processes, organizational functions, systems, etc.) in these studies. Through this process, we identified key 
themes of data use, with the support of representative empirical papers (Par´e et al., 2015). We considered empirical studies as our 
“data”, or “proofs” (Alvesson and K¨arreman, 2007), that facilitate and motivate critical reflection. The constructed nature of empirical 
materials enhanced our ability to “challenge, rethink, and illustrate theory” (Alvesson and K¨arreman, 2007). Second, we conducted an 
in-depth content analysis of a selection of conceptual papers in an integrative manner (refer to Appendix C) for papers selected for 
content analysis), which aimed to explore how the relevant theoretical assumptions can aid in interpretating the phenomena (Par´e 
et al., 2015). During this step, we actively engaged with existing theories, transitioning from inductive to a form of abductive research. 
This tandem approach to data and existing theories (Alvesson and K¨arreman, 2007) helped us integrate concepts and gather evidence 
to provide a comprehensive treatment of the subject (Schryen et al., 2020; Watson and Webster, 2020). This step allowed us to 
establish initial categories to formulate our typology by which we seek to define the roles of data in organizations.
Below, we report our key findings from the literature review.
A synthesis — The roles of data in organizations
We observe that data exhibit varied characteristics depending on their usage in different business contexts. To better understand 
these characteristics, we extensively reference and integrate conceptual discussions across different domains, allowing us to construct 
a coherent explanation. The theoretical framework by Orlikowski and Iacono (2001), referred to as “the views”, offers a valuable 
perspective for our investigation at a broad level. We also systematically collect and categorize attributes or characteristics of data as 
discussed in the literature, resulting in a set of meta categories within these overarching viewpoints, which we term “roles of data”.
In this section, we unpack our findings by proposing four primary roles that data play in organizations: data as a tool, data as a 
commodity, data as a practice, and data as algorithmic intelligence.1 By identifying these roles, we underscore the multifaceted use of 
data in organizational settings, acknowledging both their traditional roles as well as their evolving and transformative nature in to­
day’s data-driven business landscape.
Data as a Tool
The first important role we identify is data as a tool. This role is typically recognized when data are used to support decision-making 
processes in managerial contexts. We construct this meaning of data value from the research theme that addresses the use of data in 
decision-making.
Theme 1: informing business decision-making
Barkin and Dickson (1977) and Delone and Mclean (1992) were among the pioneers in exploring the use of data within the realm of 
human decision-making. Drucker (1999) subsequently built upon their work by defining information as “data endowed with relevance 
and purpose”. This perspective finds support among numerous scholars who conceptualize data as unprocessed, raw facts capable of 
being transformed into accurate and pertinent information (Knox, 2007). Zins (2007) maps the interrelations between data, infor­
mation, and (human) knowledge, to form a basic block of concepts in the information science field. More recently, Jones (2019) refers 
to a pyramid, “the Data/Information/Knowledge/Wisdom (DIKW) hierarchy” developed by Rowley (2007) and describes it as “one of 
the most fundamental, widely recognized and ‘taken-for-granted’ models in the information and knowledge literatures”.
Since 2010 s, many studies in the IS field started to investigate the use of data in organizations and its impact on business results. We 
present a list of empirical studies as illustrations (Table 1), which reveal that economic and social value can be gained from data 
through enhanced decision making, and more informed strategizing. Within this context, typical data use scenarios include business 
planning, product design and ideation, resource allocations, structured management, and operational decisions (Blohm et al., 2013; 
Brynjolfsson and McElheran, 2016; Shamim et al., 2019). Directly observed benefits of the use relate to the process of knowledge 
creation, absorbing, and sharing in organizations; thus data value is captured through improved decision-making quality (i.e., 
customer insights) and more structured management practices (i.e., strategic alignment within organizations) (Brynjolfsson and 
McElheran, 2016；Shollo and Galliers, 2016; Wang and Byrd, 2017). Such data use largely contribute to firm-level outcomes, such as 
market performance (i.e. sales and market value), customer satisfaction, and engagement (Bekmamedova and Shanks, 2014).

The tool view
The tool view acknowledges information technology as an “engineered artifact”, which permits the user and the organization to 
adapt the use of technology according to their specific needs and context (Orlikowski and Iacono, 2001). Just as traditional tools serve 
as the basis for organized labour, new digital tools have the potential to significantly impact and reshape labour and human activity 
(Karanasios et al., 2021). These new digital tools can alter, amplify, and transform how work is performed (Faulkner and Runde, 2019; 
Karanasios et al., 2021).
By considering data as a tool, we direct our attention to its “instrumental effects” (Orlikowski and Iacono, 2001) and its role in 
“mediation” (Vygotsky, 1999); so that the interaction between individuals (the subject) and their environment (the object) is mediated 
by tools (Vygotsky, 1999; Miettinen et al., 2009). While using data is more of a human centred activity (Knox, 2007), the user and the 
organization can vary the needs and purpose of using data, reshape the way of use, and improve the outcome (Orlikowski and Iacono, 
2001; Jones, 2019). As a result, the value of data is “subjective” and dependent on the user or designer, determined by their specific 
needs and “situated practice” (Jones, 2019). Data in this context, similar to other IT artifacts, can be considered separate from the 
organizational setting; they are “definable”, “unchanging”, and “independent” (Orlikowski and Iacono, 2001).
Data as a Commodity
The second role we identify is data as a commodity, in which data is traded or sold as goods to generate direct revenue or exchanged 
for other resources, mostly between organizations. This differentiation sets it apart from other types of data usage that are often limited 
to within an organization.
Theme 2: data trading
Yuchtman and Seashore (1967) identify that some resources are relatively “liquid” in the traditional economic sense and are readily 
exchangeable by an organization for resources of other kinds. More recently, Birch et al. (2021) argue that data can be “assetized”, and 
personal data can have measurable and legible value and be potentially converted into future revenue streams. This also applies to 
situations where data are integrated or bundled with other products or services, thereby contributing to their value-add in the 
marketplace (Wixom and Farrell, 2019).

stream of research represents an emerging but important direction, covering a diverse range of topics, including data liquidity, data 
commodity, data business models, data marketplaces, and data valuation (Xu et al., 2023).

The proxy view
The proxy view recognizes the adoption of “surrogate measures” to represent the essential attributes of objects, or as “symbols” 
(Orlikowski and Iacono, 2001). In the context of data being bought or sold, the common characteristic is the attribution of a monetary 
value during transactions.
Viewing data as a commodity, we focus on the “tradable” nature of data in several ways. First, data can be considered liquid assets 
as they can be easily converted into revenue at a low cost (Birch et al., 2021; Wixom et al., 2021; Piccoli et al., 2022). Certain types of 
data may exhibit a higher level of liquidity compared to others (Piccoli et al., 2022). Second, data is recognized as comprising distinct 
atomic components or data tokens that lend themselves to easy repurposing, recombination, and restructuring for the creation of new 
solutions (Kallinikos et al., 2013; Alfaro et al., 2019; Aaltonen et al., 2021). As such, research suggests that data possess characteristics 
as being editable, interactive, reprogrammable, distributable, and exchangeable (Aaltonen et al., 2021; Alaimo and Kallinikos, 2022). 
Third, when data are bought or sold, they are regarded separable from organizations. They can be decoupled from their hardware or 
software infrastructure, due to the separability nature of “bitstrings”, which is a type of digital object as defined by Faulkner and Runde 
(2019).

As depicted in Table 3, we have identified three clusters of studies that examine various aspects of the phenomena (despite the fact 
that the concepts in these studies are commonly interwoven) – such as data-driven organizations, and data-driven innovation or 
learning. The initial cluster of studies takes a broad view and explores the impact of business analytics or BDA on organizational 
competitiveness (Chen et al., 2015; Krishnamoorthi and Mathew, 2018; Olszak and Zurada, 2020). This line of research highlights the 
unique set of capabilities or competence that data contribute to, which forms competitive advantages of an organization (Xu et al., 
2023). The second cluster of studies focuses specifically on digital innovations, or data-driven innovation, and the role data play during 
the process. These investigations provide insights of how organizations leverage big data to enhance their innovation performance 
(Duan et al., 2020; Mikalef et al., 2019; Zolnowski, 2016). Lastly, the third cluster of studies centres on data-enabled learning, the role of 
big data in facilitating learning processes within organizations. These studies offer deeper insights into how data can be leveraged to 
improve organizational knowledge (Shollo and Galliers, 2016; Fink and Even, 2017; Ferraris et al, 2019).
Overall, these three clusters of research studies collectively enhance our understanding of how organizations harness data to drive 
learning and innovation in business management and improve the competitive advantage of an organization. When data are utilized 
for innovation and learning, particularly in areas like product design, service improvement, and process management, it typically 
follows a structured business process or workflow. This process involves ordered steps from data generation to knowledge creation 
(Shollo and Galliers, 2016; Grover et al., 2018), or improving their services by leveraging their data network effect in a platform (Hagiu 
& Wright, 2020). By adhering to this practice, organizations can sustain their competitiveness in products and services (Wade and 
Hulland, 2004; Grover et al., 2018).
The synergy between BDA and various other resources and capabilities within an organization introduces a novel strategy for 
maintaining a competitive edge (Aral and Weill, 2007; Grover 2018). Specifically, how data is instrumented and used, as well as the 
support provided by infrastructure and management systems, significantly impact results. In the context of organizational learning, 
Shollo and Galliers (2016) emphasize the significance of two key abilities: the capacity to initiate problem articulation and 
constructive discussions, as well as the skill to curate data effectively. As such, the “three gears” – data, insights, and actions – work 
together to generate strategic business value through BDA development and implementation (Grover et al., 2018).
The ensemble view
The ensemble view emphasizes the interactions and relationships between IT artifacts and the groups involved in their con­
struction, implementation, and use (Orlikowski and Iacono, 2001). Scholars emphasize that learning and innovation are social and 
cultural phenomena in nature, not just individual mental processes (Gherardi, 2000). Learning through participation, or learning by 
doing, highlights that learning occurs within everyday practices, even without our conscious awareness. Gherardi (2000) argues that 
knowledge is “neither in the head nor as a commodity”, but “being in the world” whereby subjects (people) and objects (knowledge) 
are “indistinguishable”, existing within ongoing action and interaction.
In the context of digital innovation and learning, we consider data as an organizational practice, by identifying their distinctive 
characteristics in three aspects. First, data represents complex interactions between people and technology, emphasizing “doing” 
rather than “being” (Orlikowski and Iacono, 2001). This is because, from a practice perspective, “knowledge [generation] is dynamic, 
provisional, and continually reproduced and negotiated through participation”; it is mediated by data, and rooted in contextual in­
teractions (Gherradi, 2000). However, it is worth noting that data-enabled learning now surpasses the traditional learning-by-doing 
notion in which humans have more control. In large digital platforms, the data network effect emerges and drives outcomes through

Theme 4: automating and algorithmic modelling
The first serious thinking about AI is often traced back to the Dartmouth summer workshop in 1956, where McCarthy and col­
leagues defined the project as making a machine behave intelligently like a human (Berente et al., 2021). They believed that all aspects 
of intelligence, including learning, could be “formalized and reproduced in a machine”. This notion has later been widely cited and 
claimed (Dick, 2019). Recent definitions of AI emphasize its ability to learn (Castelvecchi, 2016), or its capability of mimicking human 
capabilities and skills (Brynjolfsson and Mitchell, 2017). Today the vision of the field of AI goes beyond acting as a decision support 
system for human work. Instead, it advances to create artificial agents to replace human labour (Schuetz and Venkatesh, 2020). AI can 
act on behalf of humans by employing natural language processing chatbots, intelligent agents, and machine learning to interact with 
employees and customers (Davenport et al., 2018). A recent example is ChatGPT, which utilizes deep learning algorithms to generate 
human-like responses to natural language inputs (Radford et al., 2019).
Observed as an emergent topic, recent research has identified that AI presents unprecedented opportunities for organizations in 
improving productivity, creating intelligent products, and enhancing services. The typical use of AI in organizations involves two main 
types: process automation and algorithmic decision-making (ADM). Process automation is the most common business need fulfilled by 
AI, i.e., utilizing robotic process automation to automate administrative and financial tasks, both in digital and physical realms 
(Davenport et al., 2018). Moreover, algorithms play a vital role in processing rules and making decisions without human intervention. 
Powerful algorithms are built and deployed to work autonomously on behalf of humans and make decisions (Newell and Marabelli, 
2015). In certain cases, automated decision-making algorithms can override human decisions (Enholm et al., 2021; Galliers et al., 
2017; Shollo et al., 2022). However, such usage can lead to unintended consequences, including discrimination and social inequality, 
which can have harmful effects on society (Rinta-Kahila et al., 2021). To address these concerns, data curation and human oversight 
are essential to ensure the effectiveness and appropriateness of AI-driven outcomes (Sadiq et al., 2022).
The computational view
The computational view recognizes the ability of IT artifacts in representing, manipulating, storing, retrieving, and transmitting 
information (Orlikowski and Iacono, 2001). Technology can be represented here through algorithmic endeavors to build new or 
enhance existing computational systems that can support some human activity (Orlikowski & Iacono, 2001). Earlier, Zuboff (1985)
proposed two faces of “intelligent” technology — “automate” and “informate”—and their respective implications for organizations. IT 
can be applied to automate operations, substituting human labor and expertise with technology. This allows the replication of human 
labour at a reduced cost and with more control and continuity. Technology can also simultaneously generate information about the 
automated tasks, a process referred to as “informate”.
In a recent MIS Quarterly editorial article on the adoptions of AI in organizations (Berente et al., 2021), the editors argue that AI is 
not simply a technology or a defined set of technologies. Instead, it represents “an ever-evolving frontier of computational ad­
vancements that references human intelligence in addressing ever more complex decision-making problems”. Noting there remains a 
lack of precision in the conceptualization of AI, “the openness and uncertainty surrounding its meaning and usage actually provides 
greater flexibility for theoretical exploration of AI and its management” (Berente et al., 2021).

A typology and its application
Typology Description
This section provides an integrated view towards the roles of data in organizations. Table 5 presents detailed descriptions of these 
roles with a typology, which is achieved through extracting a common set of attributes across the categories, and further abstracted to 
provide a higher-order theoretical structure (Par´e et al., 2015). This typology helps capture the multifaceted data practices in orga­
nizations and identify key aspects to observe or manage. Through our synthesis, several recurring first-order constructs emerge as the 
defining dimensions to differentiate these types:
First, the purpose of data use is observed as a determinant factor to differentiate the roles. We delineate the diverse business ob­
jectives that data serve in an organization and encapsulate them using concise keywords such as informing, trading, enabling, and 
automating. These distinct purposes represent the key themes, as set out above, that data are used for in organizations; they influence 
the resultant business impact or value, as well as the way they are constructed, instrumented, or used. Second, value creation pathways or 
mechanisms can also set these roles apart. While many data-use cases in the literature identify a common set of business outcomes such 
as improved market value and productivity, which may lack a link to the use contexts, our focus centres on a more directly observable 
array of benefits arising from specific usage. This includes enhancing the effectiveness of decision-making, generating revenue, 
optimizing processes, and automating. These directly observable benefits are essential to understand value creation pathways or 
mechanisms with more nuanced, disaggregated contexts. To some extent, they may help frame the process of creating internal and 
competitive value, which is regarded as unexplained in the IS value research (Schryen, 2013). Third, the interplay between people, 
technology, and organization, can diverge across each role. For instance, data serve as a tool to inform business decisions, underscoring 
the human-centric nature of the decision-making process. Here, the value of data predominantly hinges on the end-users. In contrast, 
data can assume the form of algorithmic intelligence, where algorithms play a substantial role in generating value, and the interaction 
between individuals and technology becomes more centred around machines rather than humans. Similarly, data can be likened to a 
commodity, signifying their potential to be separated from an organization, decontextualized, and then recontextualized. Conversely, 
when data is viewed as a practice, it becomes deeply embedded within the organizational fabric, rendering it less separable from the 
organizational context. Fourth, the characteristics of data exhibit distinct facets when examined through the lens of different roles. These 
characteristics may be drawn from broader categories, such as IT artifacts or digital artifacts, or they may possess unique attributes and 
qualities stemming from the specific data use cases. These distinguishing traits are instrumental in our classification of these roles.
In light of these observations, we have drawn together and synthesized concepts and evidence from different parts of the literature 
to create a more coherent framework (Cornelissen, 2017) – a typology to define four major roles of data (Table 5). Through the 
development of this typology, the effort is aimed to illustrate “how phenomena can hang together” (Sandberg and Alvesson, 2021). 
According to Cornelissen (2017), a typology typically interrelates different dimensions to flesh out a novel explanation with new 
constructs or casual relationships which pushes our thinking about a subject.

demonstrate evolving trends. Topics regarding the tool role of data remained relatively stable with minor fluctuations during the years. 
In contrast, the practice perspective witnessed a significant rise starting from 2015, reaching its peak in 2022. The emergence of the 
commodity role has been more pronounced in the recent years, especially after 2015. AI-related discussions displayed a consistent 
upward trend, particularly after 2020. In general, there was an overall increase in the number of studies conducted, reflecting a 
growing recognition of the importance of data and their diverse applications within organizational contexts.
Discussion
Implications for Research
Given that we have extensively integrated concepts and evidence across domains, here we explain how our findings engage with 
some scholarly discussions.
First, the concept of “intelligent” technology has primarily revolved around the capabilities to “automate” and “informate” for a 
few decades since it was first proposed by Zuboff (1985). By identifying novel yet significant roles that data play, we propose an 
expansion of this notion. This entails their capacities for “informing,” “trading,” “enabling,” and “automating”, both within and across 
organizations. This expansion of capacities has been supported by robust evidence that we have drawn from the literature. Moreover, 
some characteristics distinctly set data apart from other IT artifacts, not only by their usage, effect, and character, but also in the way 
data interact with people and organization. Notably, some emergent roles of data, i.e., data as a commodity or as algorithmic intel­
ligence exhibit a reduced dependence on human orchestration in the value creation trajectory and develop an increased objectivity in 
their quality during transitions. This increased objectivity allows for greater possibility to decouple data from the organizational 
context, which, in turn, enhances the adaptability of data for reuse and repurposing (Günther et al., 2022).
Second, our research introduces fresh perspectives to the central tenets of IT/IS value research, specifically in how we define and 
perceive the value of data. We contribute to the literature by demonstrating that the purpose of data use determines the role data play 
in organizations and the value they create. While the conventional understanding in the field has largely centered on data as a resource, 
which provides an overarching explanation of their impact on business outcomes, our findings add more nuanced, fine-grained 
contexts in which data value is generated and captured. “The many faces of data”, as we have depicted, provides explanations for 
the dynamic, diverse, and complex nature of data value. Through this research, we identify that it is imperative to avoid generalizing or 
directly transferring assumptions about data from the IT/IS value research. Our research underpins the scholarly assertions that the 
value of data should not be perceived as fixed or unchanging akin to other IT assets (Aaltonen et al., 2023). Instead, the actual usage of

Implications for Practice
Defining the versatile roles data play in organizations and highlighting their distinctions holds significant meaning for practice. 
Each of the four roles have important implications. Below, we highlight a few of these implications for organizations. In doing so, we 
intend to shine a light on how the distinctions in each role can reshape the ways how organizations utilize data and assess their value. 
Then we offer our perspective what, collectively, these roles mean for organizations in the way they manage data and why an 
overarching data strategy is important.
Through leveraging different roles of data, organizations have the potential to reshape and expand the avenues through which they 
utilize data for diverse business objectives. These benefits include enhancing managerial decision-making, generating direct revenue 
by trading data, driving innovation, and automating business processes. It is important to recognize that these roles vary not only in the 
way they create value but also in the nature of use. Therefore, each role may demand a different set of supportive organizations 
capabilities. The tool role of data emphasizes the individuals using them, so organizations must focus on developing data related 
skillsets and competencies (Grover et al., 2018): for example, improving data literacy of the workforce to remove barriers in using data. 
The practice role of data emphasizes the importance of embedding data within a repeatable process and formalizing it. People are just 
one part of this ensemble; more importantly, it is the repeatable practice that embeds data within continuous business processes, 
ensuring sustainable competitive advantages for organizations (Grover et al., 2018). Useful practices include building centralized data 
platforms to democratize enterprise data to a broader audience, enabling greater access by a wide range of users, both experts and non- 
experts (Labadie et al., 2020; Lefebvre et al., 2021). These practices help reduce potential barriers in individuals’ competency or 
restrictions in data access.
In addition, attention should also be drawn to the emergent roles of data that arise from their diverse and continuously evolving 
usages in contemporary organizations. The commodity role of data is characterized by its tradable nature, indicating that data should no 
longer been seen as a cost for organizations but a source of profit. In fact, data can be considered one of the most liquid assets that an 
organization possesses (Piccoli et al., 2022). Therefore, commodifying an organization’s data assets has emerged as a strategically 
promising business model. Nowadays, data marketplaces have become readily available, and buying and selling processes have been 
extensively discussed, making data trading more practical than ever before. Data as algorithmic intelligence opens an unimaginable 
potential of data in replacing human labour and enhancing our ability in solving complex problems. To effectively leverage value from 
AI, organizations should focus on developing AI-related capabilities. This involves integrating AI initiatives with existing organiza­
tional capabilities – such as talent, management support, and process management – to create new and complementary human­
–machine capabilities (Krakowski et al., 2023; Shollo et al., 2022). At the same time, due to the nature of inscrutability in AI 
applications, organizations must address the risks associated with AI by developing control systems to mitigate potential harms and 
ensure ethical and responsible AI deployment (Berente et al., 2021; Rinta-Kahila et al., 2021).
Collectively, while grasping the unparalleled opportunities that data offer, it also introduces new challenges, urging organizations 
to adapt their strategies in data monetization, management, and governance. First, since each of these roles differs significantly in 
terms of its use context and the value creation pathways, we contend that a generalized approach to data monetization will not be 
effective. Instead, organizations should develop a comprehensive data strategy that addresses diverse sources of data value and the 
unique pathways to unlock the specific value associated with each role. This involves creating tailored business models and effectively 
linking, selecting, and allocating organizational resources to support the strategic purposes of monetizing data.
Second, recognizing the nuanced characteristics and contexts of data within organizations is instrumental in driving the reconfi­
guration and transformation of their dynamic capabilities. Data-driven capabilities have been widely recognized as a primary source of 
competitive advantage and have demonstrated the potential to yield superior performance. The development of organizational ca­
pabilities requires effective resource allocation, which entails both formal and informal processes (Dosi, 2000). Organizations also 
need to upskill their workforce in data literacy and provide supporting organizational mechanisms to effectively support the strategic 
objective of building data-related capabilities (Aral and Weill, 2007; Grover et al., 2018).
Third, developing effective data governance strategies is equally vital for organizations to address potential risks and ethical 
concerns. These strategies must strike a balance between safeguarding data privacy and security while facilitating the process of 
capturing, using, and reusing data within and across the organization(s). On one hand, establishing clear processes and policies that 
cover the entire data lifecycle, from creation or acquisition to storage or consumption, is crucial. This comprehensive approach en­
hances information resilience and enables organizations to promote data consistency, eliminate data silos, and facilitate efficient data- 
driven value creation in business management (Surbakti et al., 2020; Sadiq et al., 2022). On the other hand, data privacy and security 
demand attention, particularly in the context of data reuse via sharing and selling (Surbakti et al., 2020). While social license provides 
a guardrail when the human involvement is low (Sadiq et al., 2022), regulatory considerations are equally important to legitimize the 
use of data in AI initiatives (Gregory et al., 2021).
In brief, organizations require an integrated data strategy that not only harnesses the potential of data but also sustains data-related 
capabilities and safeguards legitimate data use. This comprehensive approach ensures that data value is fully utilized, and organi­
zations are prepared for long-term success while maintaining ethical and responsible data use.

Limitations and Future Research Opportunities
We acknowledge that there are limitations to our research. First, our typology hinges on the intangible nature of data within the 
business contexts and organizational systems within which data are operated. This means we pay less attention to ontological ques­
tions, such as their technical properties as an “entity”; epistemological questions, such as the approach of acquisition of scientific 
knowledge; or semantic questions, such as the meaning of word “data” in different contexts and situations (Gregor, 2006; Sandberg and 
Alvesson, 2021). As discussed by Aaltonen et al. (2023), these perspectives pose multi-faceted complexity for both research and 
practice to address the role of data in organizations. IS research needs to “be open to accommodating different perspectives in 
conceptualizing data as no single definition will be able to account for the complex entanglements of data with economic and social 
life”. Second, because our investigation focuses on the social context and interactions in organizations, we have not covered enough 
about the engineering-oriented conception of data. This may limit the applicability of our findings in the real-world business man­
agement context in which we acknowledge technical aspects of data are equally important to consider.
Last, while we have focused on the strategic value of data in organizations and have demonstrated their positive impact on 
organizational outcomes (as suggested by most of the literature), we also recognize that data have other aspects. Earlier, Feldman and 
March (1981) argue that organizations tend to systematically gather more information than they use, driven by the symbolic sig­
nificance of data rather than its instrumental value. It is not uncommon to see some organizations gather large amounts of data and 
generate reports because it is expected or because it projects an image of “intelligence” (Feldman and March, 1981), even if the data is 
rarely used to inform decisions. Recently, Ess´en et al. (2022) explore how business users ignore data that they themselves produced. 
This behavior underscores the ritualistic and symbolic use of data, which often results in data being treated as a byproduct or even a 
waste of organizational resources. While this cluster of interesting discussions can be potentially addressed by the nominal view of 
Orlikowski and Iacono’s (2001) framework, we suggest a deeper investigation is needed to explore these “dark sides” (Rana et al., 
2022) of data and their implications for organizational practices.
Briefly, we discuss opportunities for future research. The typology of data we derive in this paper will open a new area of research in 
the field of Information Systems. First, by understanding the “many faces of data” in organizations, the value of data should not be 
measured and generalized in a blanket manner, rather it should be contextualized based on specific use purposes. By gaining a more 
nuanced understanding of data use, researchers can advance the theoretical connection between data use and value. This will enable us 
to determine the most effective methods for utilizing and managing data in contemporary organizations. Second, the four types of data 
roles we conclude in this paper do not seem to have clear-cut boundaries in the way they are performed in practice; the context of use, 
and the value they represent, can be interwoven in many organizational practices. For example, when data are used as a tool to support 
business decision-making, a repeatable process of data-driven decision making can contribute to building a data-related organizational 
capability that is sustainable. Likewise, a repeatable process of preparing data, trading data as commodity, or a recuring practice 
utilizing data in algorithmic decision-making, not only requires an organizational capacity across many business functions to support, 
but also contributes to forming a unique set of organizational capabilities. There appears to be an interchanging value flowing between 
the four data roles, which needs to be further addressed by future research.
In conclusion, we have undertaken a comprehensive synthesis to organize existing literature and provide an integrated view of the 
roles data play in organizations. Our investigation centres on the past two decades, a period characterized by the most dynamic 
evolutions in which organizations have gone through different stages of data adoption to support their business goals, ranging from 
“static” data to BDA and AI. We present a synthesis of the characteristics of data that correspond to data usage in organizations. By 
bridging these dimensions, we propose a typology to conceptualize four major roles of data, which also holds practical relevance for 
application.
